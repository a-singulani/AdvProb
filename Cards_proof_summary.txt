Theorem 1.6 (Cond. exp.) — Existence for X≥0: key construction?	Define Q(F)=∫_F X dP; use Radon–Nikodym (Q≪P|_F) to get F-measurable density Z so ∫_F Z dP=∫_F X dP for all F∈F.
Theorem 1.6 (Cond. exp.) — How extend existence to integrable X?	Write X=X⁺−X⁻; apply the nonnegative case to X⁺ and X⁻ and subtract the resulting conditional expectations.
Theorem 1.6 (Cond. exp.) — Why is conditional expectation unique (a.s.)?	If Z,Z' match integrals on all F∈F then ∫_F (Z−Z')dP=0; apply to {Z−Z'>1/n} and {Z'−Z>1/n} to force both parts zero ⇒ Z=Z' a.s.
Theorem 1.8 — Why is conditional expectation linear?	aE[X|F]+bE[Y|F] is F-measurable and has correct integrals on each F∈F by linearity of ∫; uniqueness gives the identity.
Theorem 1.8 — Monotonicity proof idea (X≤Y ⇒ E[X|F]≤E[Y|F])?	Let A={E[X|F]>E[Y|F]}∈F; then ∫_A(E[X|F]−E[Y|F])dP=∫_A(X−Y)dP≤0, so P(A)=0.
Theorem 1.8 — Tower property proof idea (G⊂F)?	Both E(E[X|F]|G) and E[X|G] are G-measurable and match integrals on all G∈G ⇒ equal a.s. (uniqueness).
Theorem 1.8 — “Pulling out what is known” proof strategy?	Prove for indicators Z=1_{F0} by rewriting integrals over F∩F0; extend to simple functions; then to nonnegative measurable by monotone/dominated convergence; finally to signed Z via Z⁺−Z⁻.
Theorem 1.8 — Independence trick (X ⟂ F): why E[X|F]=E[X]?	For F∈F: ∫_F X dP = E[X1_F]=E[X]P(F)=∫_F E[X] dP; constant E[X] satisfies defining property.
Theorem 1.9 — Best L² approximation: why is E[X|F] the minimizer?	Error X−X0 has conditional expectation 0 given F ⇒ orthogonal to all F-measurable L² vars; Pythagorean identity gives unique minimizer at X0=E[X|F].
Theorem 1.10 — Jensen (conditional) proof idea?	Convex g is supremum of supporting affine functions ax+b; apply conditional expectation to get E[g(X)|F]≥aE[X|F]+b; take supremum ⇒ ≥g(E[X|F]).
Lemma 1.11 — Conditional density formula: what’s the key step?	Use f_{X,Y}=f_{X|Y}f_Y and Fubini: ∫h(x,y)f_{X,Y}dxdy = ∫(∫h(x,y)f_{X|Y}(x|y)dx) f_Y(y)dy; inner integral gives the conditional expectation as a function of y.
Theorem 2.4 — RCD for real-valued Y: how start the construction?	For each rational r define F(r,ω)=P(Y≤r|F)(ω); modify on countably many null sets to enforce cdf properties in r.
Theorem 2.4 — How extend the RCD identity from (-∞,r] to all Borel sets?	Check the identity on the generating class (-∞,r]; then use a monotone-class argument to extend to all Borel sets.
Theorem 2.8 — Why does “Polish ⇒ Borel space” hold (idea)?	Every Polish space is standard Borel: it is Borel-isomorphic to a Borel subset of a compact metric space / of ℝ (via coding).
Theorem 2.10 — RCD for Borel-valued Y: proof blueprint?	Use measurable isomorphism ξ:E→B⊂ℝ; push Y to ξ∘Y, get real-valued RCD, then pull kernel back via ξ^{-1}; defining identity preserved since {ξ∘Y∈ξ(A)}={Y∈A}.
Theorem 2.11 — Integral representation of E[f(Y)|F]: how prove?	First do f=1_A (RCD definition), extend to simple functions by linearity, then to nonnegative measurable by monotone convergence; signed via f⁺−f⁻.
Theorem 2.12 — Disintegration lemma: where does κ(x,·) come from?	Take RCD of second coordinate given σ(X) on product space, then factor the σ(X)-measurable object through X to get κ(x,·) measurable in x.
Theorem 2.12 — Disintegration uniqueness idea?	If two kernels integrate the same over all A∈E, they agree μ_E-a.e. on a countable generator; monotone-class extends equality to all B.
Theorem 3.7 — Snell envelope recursion: why max{stop, continue}?	At time n−1 you either stop for Z_{n−1} or continue for E[U_n|F_{n−1}]; taking the better yields U_{n−1}=max{Z_{n−1},E[U_n|F_{n−1}]}.
Theorem 3.7 — Why is the first-hitting-time rule optimal?	T*_n=min{k≥n:U_k=Z_k} is a stopping time (event described via adapted U−Z); at first hit recursion is tight, making backward induction exact ⇒ optimal.
Proposition 3.9 — Why is Snell envelope the minimal dominating supermartingale?	Recursion gives U supermartingale and U≥Z. If M is supermartingale with M≥Z, optional sampling ⇒ M_n ≥ ess sup_T E[Z_T|F_n]=U_n.
Theorem 3.10 — Markovian representation U_n=V_n(X_n): why?	Backward induction: if U_n is function of X_n, Markov property makes E[U_n|F_{n−1}] a function of X_{n−1}; thus each U_n depends only on current state.
Theorem 3.10 — Bellman recursion proof idea?	Insert Snell recursion and replace conditional expectation by integral against the transition kernel to get the dynamic programming/Bellman equation.
Proposition 3.12 — Domination upper bound: how used?	If Y dominates Z then Z_T≤Y_T; since Y supermartingale, optional sampling gives E[Y_T]≤E[Y_0]; hence sup_T E[Z_T]≤E[Y_0].
Theorem 3.14 — Martingale optimality principle: core logic?	Snell envelope is smallest dominating supermartingale; thus gives an upper bound on all stopping rewards; the hitting-time T* attains equality, hence is optimal.
Theorem 3.15 — Doob decomposition: how construct M and A?	Set ΔA_n=Y_{n−1}−E[Y_n|F_{n−1}]≥0 and A_n=Σ_{k≤n}ΔA_k; then M_n=Y_n+A_n is a martingale and Y=M−A.
Proposition 3.16 — Coin-toss recursion: why averaging appears?	With state S_n=#heads, U_n=h_n(S_n); next toss is fair/independent, so E[U_n|F_{n−1}]=½h_n(S_{n−1})+½h_n(S_{n−1}+1).
Proposition 3.18 — Secretary payoff representation: why n/N factor?	If X_n≠1, success prob 0; if X_n=1, symmetry of random permutations gives chance global best is among first n equals n/N, so P(Y_n=1|F^X_n)=(n/N)1_{X_n=1}.
Lemma 3.19 — Continuation value recursion: what are U_n and c_n?	For reward Z_n=f_n(X_n): U_n=max{f_n(X_n), c_n} with c_n=E[U_{n+1}|F^X_n}; conditioning reduces to averaging over uniform relative ranks of X_{n+1}.
Theorem 3.21 — Why does an optimal threshold rule exist, with m~N/e?	Backward induction shows stopping only worth it when X_n=1 and the stop/continue condition changes once ⇒ threshold m; compare n/N vs continuation value using harmonic sums ⇒ m≈N/e and success ≈1/e.
Theorem 4.3 — Conditional Gaussianity (informal): why stable over time?	Induct on t: affine transforms of conditionally Gaussian vectors plus independent Gaussian noise stay Gaussian; conditioning one block of jointly Gaussian on the other preserves Gaussianity.
Theorem 4.5 — Conditional mean in Gaussian regression: why pseudoinverse?	For jointly Gaussian (X,Z), conditional mean is regression projection of X onto affine span of Z; Moore–Penrose pseudoinverse handles singular Σ_ZZ while still giving the correct orthogonal projection.
Theorem 4.5 — Conditional covariance formula: what’s the interpretation?	Σ_{X|Z}=Σ_XX − Σ_XZ Σ_ZZ^+ Σ_ZX, i.e., “unexplained covariance” after removing the part explained by linear regression on Z.
Lemma 4.7 — Conditional Gaussian regression given G: proof trick?	Work under RCP P(·|G)(ω); pointwise (X,Z) is ordinary jointly Gaussian so apply Theorem 4.5, then reinterpret results as conditional expectations/covariances.
Proposition 4.8 — Generalized Kalman update: where does it come from?	Apply Gaussian conditioning/regression (Theorem 4.5) to the conditionally Gaussian pair (θ_{t+1}, ξ_{t+1}) given past observations; mean updates via gain×innovation, covariance via regression residual.
Theorem 5.4 — From disturbance representation to transition kernel: how?	Q_n(·|x,a) is the pushforward of disturbance law Q^Z_n under z↦T_n(x,a,z).
Theorem 5.4 — From transition kernel to disturbance representation: simplest choice?	Take disturbance space Z=E and set T_n(x,a,z)=z; then the kernel is recovered exactly.
Theorem 5.13 — Why doesn’t history dependence improve control value?	Markov property: given current state x_n, future law and expected reward depend on past only through x_n; replace any history-dependent policy by a Markov policy with same continuation value.
Lemma 6.3 — Monotonicity of Bellman-type operators: key reason?	If v≤w pointwise, integrating against a probability kernel preserves inequality, so L_n v≤L_n w; same for T^f_n and for T_n as supremum over actions.
Theorem 6.4 — Reward iteration under fixed Markov policy: what identity drives it?	Split reward into current + future and condition on next state: V^π_n = T^{f_n}_n V^π_{n+1}; iterating backward yields composed-operator formula.
Theorem 6.6 — Verification theorem: why do Bellman inequalities give an upper bound?	Backward induction plus monotonicity shows any v_n satisfying Bellman-type inequalities dominates the value obtained by any policy ⇒ v_n≥V_n.
Theorem 6.6 — Verification theorem: how do stage-wise maximizers yield optimality?	If f*_n attains the Bellman operator at each stage, the constructed policy achieves the upper bound everywhere, so v_n=V_n and the policy is optimal.
Theorem 6.8 — Structure theorem: why does Bellman equation hold?	Backward induction: terminal value is g_N; one-step optimization gives V_n=T_n V_{n+1} for earlier stages.
Corollary 6.9 — Dynamic programming with intermediate time m: key split?	Decompose total reward into explicit part from n to m−1 plus optimal continuation value V_m(X_m); then take supremum over pre-m policies.
Lemma 7.10 — Why do special cylinders form an algebra?	Ω is a special cylinder; complements remain finite-coordinate conditions; unions stay finite-coordinate after enlarging the finite index set.
Lemma 7.11 — Why do special cylinders generate the product Borel σ-algebra?	Product σ-algebra is generated by finite-coordinate projections; finite rectangles generate finite-product Borels, and these rectangles are exactly the special cylinders.
Lemma 7.13 — Inner regularity on Polish spaces: what property is used?	Radon property: Borel probability measures on Polish spaces are inner regular by compact sets, so μ(A)=sup{μ(K):K⊂A compact}.
Theorem 7.15 — Daniell–Kolmogorov existence: how define the pre-measure?	Set μ on cylinder sets via the prescribed finite-dimensional laws; consistency makes this well-defined and finitely additive.
Theorem 7.15 — Daniell–Kolmogorov extension: what ensures σ-additivity?	Tightness/inner regularity on finite-dimensional Polish spaces prevents mass loss on decreasing cylinder sequences; then Carathéodory extends to the full σ-algebra.
Theorem 7.15 — Daniell–Kolmogorov uniqueness: why immediate?	Cylinder sets generate B(E^T); two measures agreeing on cylinders must coincide on the whole σ-algebra.
Theorem 7.18 — Existence in Lusin spaces: proof transport idea?	Use measurable isomorphism to a Borel subset of a Polish space; push forward laws, apply Daniell–Kolmogorov there, then pull back to the Lusin space.
Proposition 8.2 — Brownian mean/cov: why is E[B_t]=0 and Cov(B_s,B_t)=min(s,t)?	Increments have mean 0 so E[B_t] constant and B_0=0 ⇒ 0; write B_t=B_s+(B_t−B_s) with independent increment mean 0 ⇒ E[B_s B_t]=E[B_s^2]=s=min(s,t).
Proposition 8.3 — Why does cov min(s,t) characterize Brownian motion (Gaussian case)?	Centered Gaussian processes are determined by covariance; min(s,t) gives Var(B_t−B_s)=t−s and zero covariance for disjoint increments; for Gaussians, zero cov ⇒ independence.
Theorem 8.4 — Brownian construction via Haar series: what’s the main definition?	Expand B(t)=Σ_{n≥0} Σ_{k=1}^{2^n} Z_{k,n} f_{k,n}(t) using Haar basis primitives f_{k,n} and i.i.d. standard Gaussians Z_{k,n}.
Theorem 8.4 — Why are sample paths continuous in the Haar construction?	Sup-norm bounds on f_{k,n} make the series uniformly convergent a.s. (via summability + Borel–Cantelli/maximal estimate), so the limit path is continuous.
Theorem 8.4 — Why does the Haar construction have covariance min(s,t)?	Independence/centering gives Gaussianity; orthonormality of the Haar basis in L²([0,1]) yields E[B_s B_t]=min(s,t).
Proposition 9.1 — Why is the exit time from (a,b) finite a.s.?	One-dimensional Brownian motion is recurrent, so it exits any bounded interval almost surely.

Theorem 9.14 — Reflection principle: why does reflecting after H_a preserve law?	After hitting time H_a, strong Markov says future is fresh BM from a; use symmetry to flip sign, so reflected path has same finite-dimensional laws as BM.

Lemma 10.8 — Why do closed sets determine a probability measure on metric space?	Equality on closed sets ⇒ equality on open sets by complements; monotone-class/Dynkin argument extends to all Borel sets since closed sets generate the Borel σ-algebra.
